About

I'm a PhD student in the Department of Civil and Environmental Engineering at Rice University, where I work in the Mobility-X Lab with Dr. Xinwu Qian. I build analytical pipelines that integrate large-scale mobility, economic, environmental, and demographic datasets to model non-technical risks in infrastructure deployment. My work spans causal inference, geospatial analytics, ML, and statistical modeling with a focus on how infrastructure shapes urban ecosystems (neighborhood structures, mobility patterns, business dynamics) and gets shaped by them.

Graduate Research Assistant 2024 – Present
Rice University, Dept. of Civil and Environmental Engineering · Houston, TX

Graduate researcher for NSF funded Strengthening America's Infrastructure, building end to end data pipelines integrating economic, environmental, demographic, and public perception datasets to model risks in energy infrastructure deployment.

  • Non-Technical Risk Modeling for Infrastructure Siting: Covering ~50% of the national charging stations, quantified how economic, environmental, and public acceptance factors create hidden deployment risks: proximity alone fails to predict infrastructure usage, and closing access gaps requires 2.6 km additional travel burden on specific communities.
  • Social Influence on Infrastructure Adoption: Nonlinear causal discovery across Seattle, WA to understand how exposure to users and infrastructure jointly determine EV adoption. Found that infrastructure acts as an amplifier (41.8% mediated effect), with adoption shaped more by social exposure than functional utility. Uniform expansion gains 1.5% adoption but widens the community gap (3.6x).
  • Economic Resilience and Market Disruption Modeling: Analytical pipelines on how infrastructure disruptions cascade into local economic impacts, covering 100% of CA commercial network. Revealed that commercially dependent communities experience 28% to 43% greater revenue exposure.
  • Urban Mobility Disruption Detection: End-to-end urban anomaly detection (LLM RAG based attribution) identifying events (e.g., grid failures during Winter Storm Elliott) across 50 metro areas. Applied causal inference to quantify cascading impacts on mobility, economic, and community functionality, with areas in Houston remaining 35–52% below baseline 12+ months post event.
Graduate Research Assistant 2022 – 2024
Alabama Transportation Institute · Tuscaloosa, AL

Developed data-driven behavioral models and optimization frameworks for the USDOT SMART Mobility initiative, analyzing user behavior and system performance in Birmingham, AL metro area (population >1M).

  • User Behavior Prediction and Segmentation: Integrated GPS trajectories and socioeconomic attributes to predict multimodal travel behaviors and cluster users into behavioral segments, improving accessibility for underserved segments by ~12%.
  • Intervention Impact Estimation: Applied interrupted time series, difference-in-differences, and propensity score matching to isolate causal effects of service interventions, including signal priority at 10 candidate intersections that reduced system travel time by 8%.
